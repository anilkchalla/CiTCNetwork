// BACKGROUND

The Science & The Story

This module is grounded in two parallel narratives — the revolutionary science of CRISPR-Cas9 genome editing, and the landmark ethical crisis it precipitated.

🧬
The Science
CRISPR-Cas9: A Bacterial Immune System Becomes a Genome Editor

CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) was first recognized in bacteria as an adaptive immune memory — a molecular archive of past viral invaders. When scientists discovered that the Cas9 protein could be reprogrammed with a synthetic guide RNA to cut any DNA sequence, the implications were transformative.

Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier demonstrated programmable in vitro DNA cleavage by Cas9 in 2012, work for which they received the 2020 Nobel Prize in Chemistry. The system requires only two components: the Cas9 nuclease and a 20-nucleotide guide RNA (sgRNA) that matches the target sequence, plus an NGG protospacer adjacent motif (PAM) in the genome.

The resulting double-strand break is repaired by cellular mechanisms — error-prone NHEJ (causing gene disruption) or precise HDR (enabling exact corrections). This simplicity and versatility sparked a revolution across medicine, agriculture, and basic research.

Key Timeline
1987CRISPR sequences first observed in E. coli (Ishino et al.)
2007CRISPR proven to be adaptive immune memory in bacteria

2013Zhang lab — first CRISPR editing in human cells
2020Nobel Prize in Chemistry: Doudna & Charpentier
2023First FDA-approved CRISPR therapy (Casgevy, sickle cell)
⚖️
The Controversy
The CRISPR Twins: Lulu, Nana, & the He Jiankui Affair

In November 2018, He Jiankui — a biophysicist trained at Rice University and Stanford — announced at the Second International Summit on Human Genome Editing that he had used CRISPR-Cas9 to edit the CCR5 gene in human embryos, resulting in the birth of twin girls he called "Lulu" and "Nana." A third baby was reported born subsequently.

He's stated goal was to confer resistance to HIV infection by disrupting CCR5, mimicking the naturally occurring CCR5-Δ32 allele. The experiment was conducted in secret, without peer review, without adequate regulatory oversight, and with consent forms that obscured the experimental nature of the work.

Genetic analysis revealed the edits were mosaic (not uniform across all cells) and did not precisely replicate the Δ32 allele. Off-target effects were not systematically assessed. The international scientific community responded with immediate and widespread condemnation.

He Jiankui was sentenced to three years in prison in 2019. The twins and the third child carry heritable genome modifications whose long-term consequences remain unknown. The case exposed the absence of clear international governance frameworks for germline editing.

// LABORATORY WORKFLOW

Step-by-Step Protocol

Three sequential modules build toward a complete in vitro CRISPR nuclease assay. All 8 sgRNA reactions run in parallel for direct comparison.

01
PCR Amplification of CCR5 Exon 2
Amplify the 724 bp CCR5 Exon 2 fragment using genomic DNA template and gene-specific primers (HsCCR5Ex2-gen-F1 / R1). Run 32 cycles with a 62°C annealing step. Prepare master mix for 8 reactions. This PCR product serves as the target DNA substrate for the downstream nuclease assay.
50 µL reaction 32 cycles 62°C anneal 724 bp amplicon
02
Step I: dsDNA Template Generation
Anneal guide-specific oligos with the tail oligo (95°C → slow ramp at 0.1°C/s). Extend annealed duplexes using T4 DNA Polymerase at 12°C for 45 min to produce double-stranded DNA templates for in vitro transcription. Each of the 8 guides requires its own guide-specific oligo paired with a common tail oligo.
T4 DNA Polymerase 12°C · 45 min 8 templates
03
Step II: sgRNA Synthesis (In Vitro Transcription)
Transcribe sgRNAs using T7 RNA Polymerase from the dsDNA templates. Incubate at 37°C for 30–60 min (longer = higher yield). Digest template with RNase-free DNase I, then purify sgRNA with an RNA cleanup column (NEB Monarch® recommended). Optional: verify on TBE-PAGE before and after purification.
T7 RNAP · 37°C DNase treatment Column purification Optional PAGE QC
04
Step III: In Vitro Nuclease Assay
Reconstitute RNP by combining Cas9 protein + sgRNA in Nuclease Assay Buffer. Add the CCR5 PCR target DNA and incubate at 37°C for 30–60 min. Remove sgRNA with RNase A (5 min, 37°C), then degrade Cas9 with Proteinase K (5 min, 37°C). Analyze cleavage products by 6% TBE-PAGE with UV visualization.
10 µL reaction Cas9 + sgRNA RNP 6% TBE-PAGE UV transilluminator
05
Data Analysis & Mismatch Interpretation
Compare cleavage efficiency across all 8 guides — the perfect-match controls (Guide 1, Guide JK) and 6 mismatch variants. Predict fragment sizes based on cut-site positions. Analyze how mismatch position (PAM-proximal vs. PAM-distal) and number of mismatches affect cleavage. Validate predictions using Benchling's mismatch sensitivity tool.
8 sgRNA comparisons Benchling analysis PAM-proximal vs distal
// sgRNA GUIDE PANEL

The 8 sgRNA Guides

Two reference guides target distinct CCR5 Exon 2 positions. Six mismatch variants of Guide JK explore how base changes at different positions affect cleavage.

PAM Proximity & Mismatch Sensitivity

PAM-proximal mismatches (positions 11–20, near the NGG PAM at the 3' end) severely reduce Cas9 cleavage activity — this is the "seed region."

PAM-distal mismatches (positions 1–10, at the 5' end) are generally better tolerated, producing partial or near-wildtype cleavage.

5' end = PAM-distal (left) · 3' end = PAM-proximal (right, adjacent to NGG)

Example: Guide JK pos 3 (G→A in MM#1) is PAM-distal — moderate tolerance expected. Pos 9–10 (MM#5) are in the seed region, PAM-proximal — activity abolished.

// BIOETHICS & RESPONSIBILITY

Ethical Analysis

The He Jiankui case — introduced in the Background section — serves as the ethical touchstone for this module. Use the discussion questions below to critically examine each dimension of the experiment.

Target Justification

Was CCR5 disruption medically necessary for the children involved? What are the risks of CCR5 loss-of-function beyond HIV resistance? Were there safer alternatives?

Informed Consent

Germline edits affect not just the individual but all future descendants. How can meaningful consent be obtained for alterations to a genome that cannot be undone?

Off-Target Risks

Any unintended edit in a germ cell is heritable. What validation would be required before germline CRISPR editing could be considered clinically responsible?

Regulatory Oversight

He bypassed institutional review. How should international bodies coordinate oversight of technologies with heritable consequences across national boundaries?

Scientific Transparency

Results were announced via press conference before peer review. What obligations do scientists have to disclose data, especially for high-risk experiments?

Equity & Access

If genome editing becomes available, who can access it? Could it widen existing inequalities — or be weaponized to enforce genetic norms? Who decides?

Teacher's Corner

Pre-lab logistics, learning objective alignment, discussion facilitation guides, and extension activities.

📋

Pre-Lab Checklist

  • Confirm genomic DNA and PCR reagent stocks
  • Order NEB HiScribe T7 Kit and Cas9 protein (allow 1 week lead time)
  • Prepare and label oligo strips for all 8 guides
  • Confirm RNase-free workspace setup and dedicated equipment
  • Cast and pre-run calibration TBE-PAGE gels
  • Assign student groups (recommend 2–3 per guide)
  • Pre-read Pieczynski & Kee (2021) — share with students week prior
  • Assign Benchling tutorial or CRISPOR practice for sgRNA design
🎯

Learning Objectives

  • Retrieve and annotate a human gene using NCBI and Ensembl
  • Design sgRNAs using in silico tools and interpret efficiency scores
  • Synthesize functional sgRNAs by in vitro transcription
  • Reconstitute and test Cas9 RNP nuclease activity
  • Predict and explain mismatch effects on cleavage
  • Evaluate off-target risk using computational predictions
  • Critically analyze ethical dimensions of germline genome editing
💬

Ethics Discussion Prompts

  • Should scientists be imprisoned for violating research ethics norms?
  • At what threshold of off-target risk would germline editing be acceptable?
  • Who should govern international research on heritable human modifications?
  • Is CCR5 Δ32 a "cure" or a genetic modification? Does the distinction matter?
  • How should we weigh benefits to individuals vs. societal risks of germline editing?
  • Compare: somatic vs. germline CRISPR — where should the ethical line be?
➕

Extensions & Variations

  • Vary sgRNA:Cas9 molar ratios and compare cleavage efficiency
  • Test influence of incubation time on nuclease activity (15 vs. 60 min)
  • Design novel sgRNAs for CCR5 Exon 1 or Exon 3 and compare
  • Extend mismatch panel: test all possible dinucleotide substitutions at position 9–10
  • Add Benchling computational predictions as a pre-lab deliverable
  • Connect to cell-based editing: discuss how in vitro results translate to cellular context
  • Integrate with NCBI Variation Viewer to explore known off-target sites

// KEY TERMS

Glossary

Search and explore key concepts from genome editing, molecular biology, and bioethics.

CRISPR
Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats — natural adaptive immunity in prokaryotes, repurposed as a gene editing system.
Cas9
CRISPR-associated nuclease 9. An RNA-guided endonuclease from S. pyogenes that introduces site-specific double-strand breaks in DNA.
sgRNA
Single guide RNA — a synthetic fusion of the crRNA (spacer) and tracrRNA scaffold that directs Cas9 to its target sequence.
PAM (Protospacer Adjacent Motif)
Short sequence required adjacent to the target (NGG for SpCas9). Cas9 cannot cleave without a matching PAM downstream of the target.
CCR5
C-C Chemokine Receptor 5. A GPCR expressed on T cells and macrophages. The primary co-receptor for HIV-1 R5 strain entry.
CCR5-Δ32
32-bp deletion in CCR5 Exon 1 that creates a frameshift and non-functional receptor — confers natural HIV resistance in homozygotes (~1% N. Europeans).
DSB (Double-Strand Break)
Both strands of the DNA double helix are cleaved. Cas9 creates blunt-ended DSBs 3 bp upstream of the PAM sequence.
NHEJ
Non-Homologous End Joining — error-prone DSB repair that generates small insertions or deletions (indels), often disrupting protein function.
HDR
Homology-Directed Repair — precise DSB repair using a template. Enables exact gene correction but is inefficient in most cell types.
RNP (Ribonucleoprotein)
Cas9 protein pre-complexed with sgRNA before delivery. RNP format reduces off-targets by limiting Cas9 exposure time.
Off-Target Effects
Unintended edits at genomic sites with sequence similarity to the guide RNA. PAM-proximal mismatches are most predictive of off-target activity.
In Vitro Transcription
Cell-free RNA synthesis using T7 RNA Polymerase on a dsDNA template. Used here to produce sgRNAs from oligo-generated templates.
Germline Editing
Editing in reproductive cells or early embryos, making changes heritable. Ethically distinct from somatic editing due to transmission to future generations.
Indel
Insertion or deletion of nucleotides at the CRISPR cut site, often caused by NHEJ. Frame-shifting indels disrupt protein coding sequences.
TBE-PAGE
Tris-Borate-EDTA Polyacrylamide Gel Electrophoresis — recommended for resolving small DNA fragments in this assay (6% gel for optimal resolution).
Mismatch Tolerance
The ability of Cas9-sgRNA to cleave targets with non-complementary bases. Tolerates 1–2 PAM-distal mismatches; highly sensitive to seed-region mismatches.
